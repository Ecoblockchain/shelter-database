The recommendations given below are summarizing some basic principles that should be taken into consideration during planning and implementation of shelter projects when building in disaster prone areas.  Recommendations are given for each hazard  and organized them in the same logic of layers and buidling elements that  is used to document the shelters in the database. Please note that the recommendations are not exhaustive and can only give some general guidance.

 

  Hazard Attribute Recommendations
To resist: HIGH WIND site selection Locate away from areas exposed to strong winds, such as exposed crests, coastal areas, etc.
    site Wind breakers around the shelter like vegetation up to 3m (10 ft) high can serve to protect the shelter from strong winds. The shelter should be locatd at a distance from higher trees (app. Same distance than height of the tree) to avoid that the trees fall on the shelter during a storm.
      Determine the load-bearing capacity of the soil through soil is test/analysis in order to determine the type and depth of the foundation (reaching the appropriate soil bearing capacity).
    shelter design Use appropriate building shapes, materials and detailing (e.g., building orientation and roof pitch to minimize wind load).
    orientation The shorter side of the shelter should face towards the dominant direction of strong winds.
    building shape Build simple shapes: squared; rectangular with ratio: length/width ? 1.5; round. Avoid complicated shapes, such as L-shape, T-shape, U-shape.
    construction Ensure professional site supervision to assure technically sound construction and quality of workmanship.
    foundation Make sure the foundation is deep enough to reach the soil which is appropriate for bearing the loads (good soil).
      Make sure that there is a continuous load path (interconnection) between the upper structure (walls and roof) right through to the foundation.
      Build a heavy foundation or ensure good anchorage into the ground (using screw piles or earth anchorage system) to avoid the up-lift of the shelter.
    structure Walls should be properly anchored to the foundation to avoid the overturning and sliding of the shelter.
      Walls should be braced in every direction to avoid the racking of the shelter.
    roof Roof shape: choose a shape that contributes to resisting up-lift forces, such as hip-roof, gable roof (if the shelter size is inferior to 20m2), dome, conical roof or arched roof. Avoid single-pitch roof as it does not resist well up-lift forces.
      Ensure the pitch is around 30°, in order to reduce the up-lift forces.
      Properly place and secure roof components with adequate fixings.
      Roof structure (trusses, purlins, rafters) should be properly anchored to the structure below (walls) to avoid the up-lift of the roof.
      Properly anchor or strap every element (trusses, purlins, rafters, roof decking, battens/ laths) to avoid the up-lift of the roof.
      Properly brace the roof structure.
      Eaves (rafter overhang) should be 30cm (1ft) long maximum.
      Veranda roof should be separated from the main roof.
      Use roofing screws rather than roofing nails, as they better resist up-lift forces.
    opening Should not be placed in the corner, should be at a minimum of 1.20m (4ft) from the corner to allow proper bracing of the walls.
      Should be facing each other.
      Shutters should be installed on each window.
      Openings should not be wider than 0.90m (3ft).
  EARTHQUAKE site selection Locate in low risk areas, on stable ground as far away from faultlines as possible
      Avoid construction in areas where liquefaction of soil occurs (such as near river beds, coastal areas with sandy soils and high water tables; check soil mapping of teh area if availabel or have the soil tested).
    site Make sure that the soil is tested/analysed to determine the load-bearing capacity of the soil, and then the depth of the foundation (reaching the appropriate soil bearing capacity).
    shelter design Use appropriate building shapes, materials and detailing (e.g., strengthen ring beams at corners).
    buidling shape Build simple shapes: squared; rectangular with ratio: lenght/width ? 1.5; round. Avoid complicated shapes, such as L-shape, T-shape, U-shape.
    construction Ensure professional site supervision to assure technically sound construction and quality of workmanship.
    foundation Make sure the foundation is deep enough to reach the soil which is appropriate for bearing the loads (good soil).
      Make sure that there is a continuous load path (interconnection) between the upper structure (walls and roof) right through to the foundation.
      In case of an elevated ground floor, make sure that the lower structure (stilts, pillars, wall base) is properly braced to resist lateral forces. Avoid stilts or columns of different lenghts or dimensions
    structure Walls should be properly anchored to the foundation to avoid the overturning and sliding of the shelter.
      Walls should be braced in every direction to resist lateral forces and avoid the racking of the shelter.
      If you are not in a position to calculate the section of the structural posts, at least make sure that the posts are oversized to better resist lateral forces. Example: if you usually build pillar/ concrete post 20 x 20 cm, then you should increase the section to 30 x 30 cm and increase the size of the reinforcing bars.
    roof avoid heavy roofs, e.g. concrete slabs.
      Properly place and secure roof components.
      Roof structure (trusses, purlins, rafters) should be properly anchored to the structure below (walls).
      Properly anchor or strap every element (trusses, purlins, rafters, roof decking, battens/ laths).
      Properly brace the roof structure.
      Veranda roof should be separated from the main roof.
    opening Should not be placed in the corner, should be at a minimum of 1.20m (4ft) from the corner to allow proper bracing of the walls.
      Openings should not be wider than 0.90m (3ft).
  FLOOD site selection Locate away from flood plains and locations with high water tables.
      Provide adequate drainage systems.
    site Make sure that the soil is tested/analysed to determine the load-bearing capacity of the soil, and then the depth of the foundation (reaching the appropriate soil bearing capacity).
    shelter design Raise house above ground level (if possible above the 100-year flood level).
      Reinforce ceiling for storage in mezzanine level.
    building shape Build simple shapes: squared; rectangular with ratio: length/width ? 1.5; round. Avoid complicated shapes, such as L-shape, T-shape, U-shape.
    construction Ensure professional site supervision to assure technically sound construction and quality of workmanship.
    foundation Make sure the foundation is deep enough to reach the soil which is appropriate for bearing the loads (good soil).
      Ensure good anchorage into the ground (using screw piles or earth anchorage system) to avoid the up-lift, sliding and overturning of the shelter.
    ground floor Should be above the 100-year flood level.
      Make sure that the lower structure (stilts, pillars, wall base) is properly braced to resist lateral forces.
    structure Walls should be properly anchored to the foundation to avoid the overturning and sliding of the shelter, due to the high pressure caused by water, especially flash floods.
      Walls should be braced in every direction to avoid the racking of the shelter.
    roof Properly place and secure roof components.
      Build a mezzanine up in the roof to store valuable items in case the ground floor is flooding.
      Roof structure (trusses, purlins, rafters) should be properly anchored to the structure below (walls).
      Properly anchor or strap every element (trusses, purlins, rafters, roof decking, battens/ laths) to avoid the up-lift of the roof.
      Properly brace the roof structure.
      Install a guttering system to collect the rainwater. Make sure that the rainwater evacuates towards the drainage system. This will protect the walls and foundation of the shelter.
    opening Should not be placed in the corner, should be at a minimum of 1.20m (4ft) from the corner to allow proper bracing of the walls.
      Should be facing each other, to prevent the wall from collapsing due to the high pressure caused by water.
      Openings should not be wider than 0.90m (3ft).
  TIDAL SURGE/ TSUNAMI site selection Locate away from areas vulnerable to tsunamis or storm surges.
    planning Provide clear evacuation routes, and etablish clear warning systems and evacuation plans.
    shelter design shelters cannot be designed to withstand tidal surges or tsunamis.
  LANDSLIDE site selection Locate away from areas vulnerable to landslides (e.g., on or at the bottom of steep slopes).
      Provide slope stabilising systems (e.g., retaining walls, vegetation, adequate drainage, etc.).
    shelter design shelters cannot be designed to withstand landslides.
  VOLCANO site selection Locate away from areas vulnerable to volcanoes (e.g., away from lava flows, smoke, ash and explosions).
    shelter design shelters cannot be designed to withstand volcanoes.
  FIRE site selection Plan settlements with fire breaks.
      Leave gaps between shelters to provide fire breaks (at least twice the height of the shelter).
    shelter design Use appropriate building shapes, materials and detailing (e.g., use fire retardant materials).
     

Allow space between individual shelters and any local fire hazard, such as kitchen, workshop, etc.
